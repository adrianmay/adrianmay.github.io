1 Introduction

Empirical Development is an evidence-based software development process.

The main points are:

  • The customer might be king, but don’t die for them.
  • Take control of the product roadmap.
  • Quote probabilities with all deadlines.
  • POs decide what, PMs predict when.
  • Testers are trainee POs.
  • Use data mining and simulation for estimation and planning.
  • Use the same data for detecting knowledge monopolies.
  • Don’t sprint cos it distorts, blurs and oversimplifies everything.
  • Control tech debt by not rushing the devs.
  • Involve devs in designing solutions.
  • Specify a range of luxury to allow scope trimming under pressure.
  • Minimise churn using employee engagement.
  • Foster controversy, as it’s the mother of invention.

2 Reality

2.1 Customer relationships

2.1.1 Types

  • (Time and materials out of scope.)
  • Might just be willing to do scrum, esp. small, non-technical customers.
  • Number of customers of the codebase: 1, 5 or a million: affects power over roadmap.
  • Tendering, and similar waterfall contracts.
  • Technical customers: might inspect source code.
  • Early stage start-up: collect data to get prepared for later

Badly written contracts (from flying pigs to blank cheques.)

Conspiring with the customer to underestimate costs and schedule.

Customers would always rather squeeze out another comfort feature before signing off.

Psychological abuse by customer and self-help.

BATNA

Trust is the only currency.

2.2 Stochastic implementation times

Ticket implementation times are long tailed distributions.

Guarantees about small units of work are foolhardy.

Epics more predictable than individual tickets, if you do the sums right.

Even completed tickets aren’t 100% certain, as bugs may yet be found.

Real vs imaginary deadlines: customer must quantify the damage of lateness of specific goals to set risk aversity.

2.3 Developers

2.3.1 Constraints

Will start the next ticket when the previous one is complete.

Devs can only rescue endangered deadlines by incurring tech debt.

Devs choose tickets that match their skills:

  • Personal knowledge monopolies are common.
  • Adding up story points assumes a clone army.
  • Velocity is not per team, rather, per-dev, or per-dev-in-a-given-module.

Prioritization melee:

  • Emergencies
  • Deadlines
  • Blocking and blocked tickets
  • Procrastinated tech debt
  • Pick a small ticket cos sprint end is near (what???)

Devs’ other duties

2.3.2 Psychology

Devs are in short supply, and they know it.

More likely to be turnover’s limiting factor than customers.

Depressed by tech debt and angered by being forced to incur it.

Taught to overgeneralise.

Enjoy retros.

Reluctant to estimate in days, fearing accountability.

Sociable, but need uninterrupted time.

Might fail to fear complexity.

2.4 Tech debt

It’s deadly.

It is literally a debt, so calculate the interest rate and tell the CFO.

Want to keep codebase alive until rendered obsolete by changes in market or tech.

2.4.1 The gory details of codebase collapse

(% indicates measurable symptom)

  • All productive work generates mess which costs money and time to clear up:
    • Every builder understands this, as does every restauranteur, every hairdresser, everybody.
    • Only software managers miss this because the mess is only visible to the devs.
  • There’s regular deadline pressure %
  • Devs beg for time to tidy up and are refused due to deadline pressure.
  • Smart devs ignore the refusal, but they’re outnumbered and can’t keep pace.
  • The mess complicates future development so:
    • it multiplies exponentially,
    • coding is slow, %
    • speed of coding is less predictable. %
  • Devs become despondent at the mess they have to deal with and dream of a better project.
  • They blame managament for refusing them the time to work to a standard they could be proud of.
  • They abandon ship, and especially if they’re vocal about the reasons, others follow. %
  • Newbies are hired but there aren’t enough old hands to teach them about the spaghetti.
  • A high proportion of new hires quit early. %
  • They reimplement functionality they don’t know is already there so:
    • Linecount expands faster than features. %
    • Bugs proliferate due to the redundancy.
    • Terror modules appear: those that nobody dares touch and that complicate life for interacting modules
  • More time devoted to bug fixing than new features. %
  • More deadline pressure cos feature train not advancing.
  • Bug list can’t be shortened after many months or years. %
  • Toxic blame game breaks out.
  • Investors have seen all this before and pull the plug.

Along that timeline, there’s a “technical watershed” after which there is no hope of rescuing the codebase, but before then, there’s a “political watershed” after which the decisions required to rescue it will not be made because they would be so expensive that denial is the more likely response.

2.5 Common dysfunctional mechanisms

Disappoint customer -> Intimidated by their frustration -> Unrealistic promises -> Vicious circle

Deadlines impossible -> Rushed devs -> Tech debt -> Unexpected rework -> Vicious circle

Verbose and barely understood code -> Frustrated devs -> Experts quit -> Hire newbies -> Vicious circle

Top dev made team lead -> Admin duties -> Less tech leadership

POs too busy to plan long term -> Customers request the unexpected -> Replanning -> Vicious circle

POs too busy to write good tickets -> Unforeseen edge cases -> Inaccurate estimation -> Impossible deadlines

PM/PO distinction unclear -> mixed messages to customer

No testers -> devs marking own exam papers and POs overloaded.

Top management sees only customer mood -> Tech debt.

3 What is a development process for?

3.1 Not developer choreography

They’re already constrained and will be most efficient if not molested.

Roman army chessboard formation analogy (impresses emperors and enemies, but goes to pot when the whistle blows.)

Devs must do things to support other business functions, but minimise this and be flexible.

3.2 Contract formation

Avoid blank cheques and flying pigs.

Fix the timescale (so you can book other customers in) but allow flexibility in the scope.

Only commit to the bare essentials but strive for luxury features, perhaps for a higher price.

Make sure POs and devs vouch for the feasibility and PMs have calculated the timescale or added loads of contingency.

3.3 Capacity planning

Make sure staff are available at the times they’re booked to customers.

3.4 Creating a roadmap

Important for controlling future offering.

Have one, or be steered by the customer (who doesn’t have to own your code.)

Allow for scope trimming (project management triangle.)

3.5 Predicting rates of progress

Should be a mathematically correct method based on evidence.

Scrum does not meet that standard.

3.6 Being trusted by customers

Don’t make digital promises: expose probabilities instead.

3.7 Controlling tech debt

Purge any methodology that incentivises devs to cut corners:

  • Devs should not know deadlines
    • (Assumes scope-trimming decisions made by PMs/customers (POs having set the options in advance.)
  • No sprint goals, therefore no sprints

Onus is on PMs to predict implementation times, not on devs to run after them.

Requirements gate should protect codebase.

Devs should make quantitative case for refactorings.

3.8 Retaining staff

Devs are more often the factor that limits revenue than customers.

Extreme ramp-up times for non-trivial projects, so churn is lethal.

Employee engagement.

4 Roadmap preparation by POs

Do what’s in your own interest. The customer is a means to an end.

Roadmap is:

  • Long term: problems people would like solved, no solutionizing.
  • Short term: tickets for devs.
  • Mid term: solutionizing phase: negotiation between customers and devs facilitated by PO.

Needed for:

  • Controlling long term product evolution.
  • Fending off ad-hoc customer requests.
  • Informing devs of long term requirements to support architecture decisions.
  • Staffing and capacity planning.
  • Not pitching for business you can’t deliver.
  • Flexing scope (luxury) rather than quality or schedule.

Roadmap items should offer a range of luxury levels. Customer sees roadmap and is motivated to keep pace with it, which means they agree to reduce luxury level when things take longer than expected. Option of cutting quality or magically working faster is not entertained, although postponement of whole roadmap might be.

4.1 POs

Roadmap is driven by people with domain and product knowledge.

Define POs as people with that knowledge.

Other duties requiring that knowledge (which POs will try to delegate in a later chapter):

  • Reviewing finished stories
  • Receiving customer requests and saying:
    • We have that already
    • It’s on the roadmap
    • We’ll think about it
    • Not gonna do it
  • Preparing training material

but not:

  • Estimating completion dates (that’s PMs)
  • Testing releases (that’s testers)

4.2 Solutionizing Process

Assume the roadmap is notionally ordered in all three terms. Devs will use that to make assumptions about what’s been done by the time the task of interest will be attempted.

Look for practical solutions that maximise customer satisfaction whilst minimising disruption of codebase. (Refactorings should consider entire roadmap.)

Involve all customers who might want the feature.

Involve testers who’ll ask about edge cases and force the spec to be expressed unambiguously.

Expect devs to invent simpler solutions after apparent finalisation.

Design solutions whose luxury level can be trimmed when under deadline pressure.

4.2.1 To detailed tickets

Virtues and use of Gannt charts:

  • Plot complex sequential dependencies
  • Accept more work to decouple domino runs
  • See how many devs can parallelise
  • Interpret like a weather forecast (uncertainty increases with lead time)

The art of technical writing (and chatting): see miscellaneous chapter.

4.2.2 Non-functional requirements

Everybody thinks they need 99.99% availability and CIA-proof security. Are you as safety-critical as, say, an ambulance station?

What do you get punished for? Downtime? Bugs? Neither?

What’s likely to bring you down? Internet failures, or your own bugs?

How much performance are you prepared to waste in order to cope with load?

How much would it cost to just throw money at a load problem?

Do you test for performance complexity?

5 Testers as assistant POs

POs were defined as those with domain and product knowledge.

They are very busy with roadmap preparation. Can we relieve them of the “other duties” above?

Yes, using testers, but the POs must file testable tickets. That’s also good because:

  • POs forced to think through the edge cases early
  • Devs’ estimates are better informed

Testers grow into POs cos they’re learning domain and product knowledge.

A PO with the skills and mindset of a tester would be formidable.

6 Estimation by PMs

Good estimation is the precondition of all best practices:

  • Under deadline pressure, nothing strategic gets done.
  • Under constant deadline pressure, codebase collapse is inevitable.

PM’s domain. POs not involved. PMs don’t need much domain or product knowledge, but they might find a statistics course useful.

There are no commitments. There are probabilities, which are more useful to everybody involved.

Weather forecast anecdote on the folly of digitizing probabilities.

6.1 Little Oracle

Looks at a ticket in isolation (with hypothetical field value overrides) and returns mean and variance of probability distribution of implementation time.

Made by data mining past tickets and source control.

(Measuring implementation time of past tickets is not trivial, but we can start crude and refine later.)

Likely predictive fields (this is just a guess - only the stats can decide):

  • Story points (one would hope)
  • Volatility: dev-assigned t-shirt size of variance: “beaten track” vs. “experimental” vs. “utter mystery”
  • Assignee: some are slower than others, maybe cos they’re more strategic or diligent
  • Zones (i.e. directories) of codebase: spaghetti modules
  • Familiarity of assignee in zones: mined as work done there by that dev in the recent past

(Keep richness of model within Hoeffding’s inequality; 10 * coefficients < training points.)

But how early can these fields be populated? In time for estimation purposes?

Scrum leaves assignee until the last minute, and that’s not helpful for estimation.

Little oracle can help teams plan by advising how long a ticket might take according to who attempts it.

6.2 Big Oracle

Considers a set of tickets and a release date and returns the probability of all those tickets being completed by then.

Uses little oracle (which only returns durations), but needs to know start date for each ticket.

That’s the completion date of that dev’s previous ticket, so we must know the assignment of tickets to devs and their ordering, a.k.a. their “in-trays”.

Should know about holidays and equivalent interruptions like workshops.

6.2.1 In-trays

Take an ordered backlog. Assign some tickets to some devs. Filter on an assignee, retaining order. That’s an in-tray.

“Tetris” assignment method:

  • Pull a ticket from the ordered backlog.
  • Assign it to whoever will finish it first considering:
    • how much work they already have in their in-tray,
    • how long the little oracle thinks that ticket will take them.
  • Repeat until all in-trays cover the timescale of interest for estimation.

(That makes no attempt to disseminate monopolised code knowledge, but see the chapter on skills management.)

If teams use some defined method like the above, it can be simulated or automated. Human overrides would improve the planning prognosis, so planning based on the simulation gives a valid worst case.

Sequential dependencies between tickets can be dealt with by reordering the backlog to minimise the chance of overlap. The big oracle will estimate that chance.

6.2.2 Release Planner

By a given release date, a PM may hope that each dev gets through a prefix of their in-tray.

To get the probability of success:

  • The mean implementation time of the prefix is the sum of the means of the tickets in that prefix.
    • (The truth of this is a desideratum for the method of measuring implementation time.)
  • Similarly add the variances.
  • Start from when work started on the dev’s current ticket.
  • Use normal distribution (that error is inconsequential) with that mean and variance to read off the probability of completion before the release date.
  • Treat different devs as independent and multiply the probabilities.
    • (It’s assumed that devs minimised the probability of overlapping sequential dependencies when planning, otherwise an advanced big oracle can model that probability.)

PM can mark a ticket as “promised”, which discourages teams from rearranging in-trays to the detriment of such a ticket.

Won’t-do closure is the same as completion for planning purposes.

Similar tool for fixed-scope, variable-date releases.

6.3 The World Changes

… when the PM shares the release planner with the customer and invites them to choose their own risks. (The ordering has already been agreed via POs)

The customer would initially choose too many tickets but notice the probability plummet. Now, instead of haggling oppositionally, the PM and customer are working together against the maths of life. The brutal behaviour of probabilities is made plain to both, and past failures seem more understandable. The customer is forced to decide which tickets are truly urgent and which are gold plating.

If forced to commit to a deadline, mouth the words, but state the probabilities as well. Expose changes of probability early. Gradually, they should learn that the probabilities are more helpful to them than doubtful commitments.

6.4 Huge Oracle

Sees further into the future by aggregating tickets and people.

Connects customers and suppliers in a single system where estimation is transparent.

Unified contract process for large and small scale activities.

Start with a “parent” field in tickets which identifies the ticket that motivated this one.

7 Development

Must retain devs (might take several years to ramp up newbies.)

Devs have lots to do besides chasing deadlines:

  • Meetings
  • Architecture planning
  • Bug triage and fix
  • Devops, build train
  • Customer support
  • Data curation?
  • Tech debt reduction, unit testing
  • Tech lead duties (if they exist)
  • Code reviews
  • One to ones
  • Documenting code
  • Helping newbies
  • Marking recruitment tests
  • Performance reviews
  • Guilds?

Therefore, much less than 50% of dev time will go on feature development.

7.1 Organisation

Leadership exists but is not digital.

Team boundaries become module and coding-culture boundaries. Use exchange schemes to limit cultural disconnect.

Safe and unsafe specialisation.

Testers should not be embedded in dev teams but assist POs.

Teams should be strict in policing POs into writing quality tickets.

No single tech lead. Use admin staff for admin.

Correct leadership style:

  • Challenge and support.
  • Socratic teaching method (ask, don’t tell.)
  • Focus on pivotal questions.
  • Consult knowledgeable or affected people.
  • Escalation gateway and mediator.

7.2 Rituals

Sprints are both harmful and futile, but retros are popular.

No afternoon meetings for devs - coding only. Rare exceptions tolerated.

PO time belongs to devs in the morning so they must meet customers and each other in afternoons.

7.2.1 Refinement

PO’s meet teams for regular refinement meetings which alternate with PO-customer meetings. Could have a daily slot of up to 60 minutes after standup.

Refinement demand is patchy (high when planning a big new feature) so that 60 minutes might need to be adjusted. If afternoons are to be used for workshops, tell the estimation machine about the resulting standstill using tickets or holidays.

Refined tickets have story points, volatility and code zone set. Or whatever the current estimation algorithm calls for.

Assignment doesn’t need to be set this early cos the estimation machine can simulate tetris.

Instead of story points, this process would be equally happy with devs estimating in days. Either way it’ll be calibrated. But devs feel less exposed using story points.

7.2.2 Assignment

Estimation requires per-dev in-trays, not scrumboard or kanban.

Teams have regular meetings to populate in-trays. The earliest non-closed ticket in an in-tray is implicitly in progress. Park tickets by re-ordering.

Tetris process described in estimation chapter. That’s the simplest starting point, but refinements expected.

Teams complain to POs when refined backlog in danger of running dry, or, PO directly responsible.

7.2.3 Updating tickets

In-trays (i.e. assignee and ordering) may be rearranged by teams with impunity if it wouldn’t delay estimation of a ticket marked as promised. If it would, they still can, and a PM will be alerted.

Story points, volatility and code zone should be updated by devs whenever new, relevant information comes to light. The ticket tracker must remember the full history of all fields for consumption by the estimation machine.

There could also be a percentage progress field, but we shouldn’t ask devs to update it every day, rather, it’ll count down automatically and the assignee may adjust it if it looks wrong.

PMs will be alerted if a deadline is endangered by any change (perhaps of a ticket before the promised one in the same in-tray.)

The rest of the time, the estimation machine can infer progress.

7.2.4 Stand-ups

Morning stand-ups:

  • Hard to predict how long a discussion will ramble for, so hard to decide to break-out
  • So agenda should be:
    1. Anybody blocked?
    2. Anybody know something that affects what somebody else should be doing? (Adjourn after this if over 30 minutes.)
    3. Anything to demo to PO? (Even with sprints, these should be ASAP upon completion.)
    4. Any gossip?

If somebody wants a breakout meeting that requires just a subset of a team, the standup is the least appropriate context in which to arrange it. They can arrange a meeting in the diary or chat system.

PMs may turn up to standups to ask for estimate updates on tickets.

7.2.5 Demos

If the PO doesn’t turn up regularly to standups, they are summoned by any ticket awaiting demo. Dev’s need fast feedback. POs may not book other meetings over standup time.

10 Miscellaneous Topics

10.1 Remote working

Advantages:

  • Avoid commuting overhead
  • Meetings better:
    • Less disturbance to and from uninvolved colleagues
    • Everybody can screen share
    • Mixture of f2f and remote unfair
  • Bigger job applicant pool -> can specialise in emerging tech
  • Environment

Problem:

  • No facetime -> less team spirit, lonely

Solution:

  • Let people travel for facetime activities, but not for solitary work or standard meetings

Examples of facetime events:

  • Conference format: stands where people animate discussions of topics they care about
  • Special interest groups, e.g., guilds, kill the company exercises
  • Random groupings

10.2 Architecture

Circle of life:

  • Obsolescence of whole project under market changes, hopefully caused by this project.
  • Obsolescence of codebase under tech innovations.
  • Obsolescence of modules under large refactorings.
  • Obsolescence of functions under small refactorings.

Architecture should expect the above.

Data first:

  • Is it all there, and up to date
  • Push or pull, recovery
  • Eliminate redundancy
  • Make sure everybody can draw the data model blindfold

Don’t splurge process boundaries:

  • The processes just end up modelling each other and fretting about connectivity.
  • 1 out of 10 processes down = system down.

Draw your acyclic build dependency graph.

It’s nonesense that high level interfaces can or should remain fixed.

No need to pre-empt imaginary future requirements. Keep code light and disposable instead.

Don’t write loads of code in terms of generalisations about the domain or architecture. Keep most code project-agnostic.

Only handle error cases as a last resort:

  • There might be a useful interpretation of the case
  • Rule it out in the data model
  • Don’t repeatedly check at every level of the call stack, document assumptions instead
  • Don’t check what you can’t treat: consider UDP

10.3 Hiring

Usually limited by devs, not customers, so continuously hire good ones (bar raised and lowered by budget) and train them before the customer inquiries arise.

Test for:

  • Knowledge
  • Aptitude
  • Psychology

10.4 Technical Writing

  • State context.
  • More nouns than pronouns.
  • No passive tense.
  • Indicate the question in the answer.
  • Avoid garden path sentences:
    • Don’t use words that might be nouns or verbs (except in US where there’d be none left.)
  • Suspect all generalisations, even this one.

11 Appendix: Collection of Scrum Fallacies

11.1 About customers

11.1.1 Incompatible with billing

Customers are rarely prepared to do scrum. It can only happen under time and materials billing, which implies that the customer owns the codebase, in which case, there’s no customer relationship to speak of other than some monthly timesheets. These are the circumstances under which the supplier needs no process.

Fixed price work needs a waterfall spec and timescale. Result is internal “phony scrum.”

11.1.2 Conflict of interest

Offering a customer a scrum-like cycle under a fixed price contract invites abuse. (Everything is MVP.)

11.1.3 Utility of predictions only 2-3 weeks ahead

Few customers care about that granularity, and if they micromanage the supplier to that degree, then the trust is already broken.

11.2 About the “what”

11.2.1 PO and customer dictate full spec to devs

Devs must advise about which solutions are easy

11.2.2 Recipe for tech debt

No long term plan.

No requirements gating process.

No room for refactoring.

11.2.3 Devs have last word on architecture

Try telling a computer-literate customer that.

11.3 About the “when”

11.3.1 The Sprint Commitment

Digitising probabilities is self-deception. Don’t ignore variance

Devs shouldn’t be motivated to cut corners.

11.3.2 Probability of hitting sprint goal

Managers bet the farm on doing so, but the odds are well below 50%. If loaded to average velocity and in equilibrium then:

  • Velocity is an average, so the probability of scoring fewer points equals that of scoring more.
  • If you score less, you didn’t hit the sprint goal.
  • If you score more, perhaps those were different tickets from the promised ones.

If 10 tickets in a sprint each independently have P=90% (no ticket has more), then the sprint goal is at 35%. (In fact they can only be independent if there are 10 devs. The point is that people intuitively approximate 90% to 100% and accumulate this huge error over a set of tickets.)

11.3.3 Pointing without technical analysis

Edge cases and architectural difficulties not anticipated

11.3.4 Dancing to the gong

No power in the world can make tickets start and end in sync.

But devs can fake it:

  • Close the ticket and file another for the unhandled edge cases
  • Neglect the unit tests
  • Waste time after completing tickets to hold velocity down.

11.3.5 Planned vs. surprise points

Surprise points that arise after planning:

  • Spun off from prematurely closed tickets
  • Caused when stumbling over tech debt
  • Filed by devs upon finding bugs

Velocity credits both planned and surprise points, but planners load sprints with planned points up to the velocity, only to see sprints blown by surprise points.

11.3.6 Discards true start and finish times

… so useless for mining durations.

Accident of ticket finishing just before or just after sprint end causes huge noise in velocity.

That noise means you must wait several sprints to deduce a systematic change with statistical significance.

11.3.7 Degradation of historical data

  • Implementation time records distorted by incentive to fake sprint success.
  • Story points distorted by legality of adjustment after planning.

11.3.8 No guidance on pointing under uncertainty

  • Should they go by the mean or worst case?
  • What to do with initial investigation tickets?

11.3.9Illusion that short sprints ought to be predictable

Russian roulette is equally dangerous however often you count the score.

11.3.10Specialisation ignored under clone-army assumption

Happy to load sprint entirely with tickets only one dev can do

Velocity is just per-team although teams maximise their ability gradient (every team needs a top gun and must share newbie load.)

To believe that lucky and unlucky tickets balance out in a sprint, you have to believe that a lucky dev can add labour to an unlucky ticket without friction, which is to deny that a dev is more expert than others at the very ticket they have in progress.

11.3.11 Story points as unit of time

They claim story points are about complexity rather than time, but they use it like time anyway when loading sprints.

Days in terms of points is not a y=mx+0 function.

11.4 About the lifestyle

11.4.1 Sanctity of the sprint

  • People have to put fires out.
  • A ticket in progress might need another team to flip a switch (before 1-2 sprints pass.)
  • Tickets get blocked and go on hold.
  • Tech debt is uncovered.
  • Ideas change.
  • Nightclub rules is just bureaucratic life-support for a failed tracking method.

11.4.2Desire for linearly descending burndown

Why? A steady state is preferable.

11.4.3 Prioritise the small ticket near sprint end

Absurd usurping of priorities

11.4.4 Subdivide tickets to fit in sprint

This is just for scrum’s sake but it clouds the relationship between tickets and deliverables.

There are natural divisions, but they often only become clear during coding.

11.4.5 Protecting devs from blame.

Blame is not what worrys them. Tech debt is.

11.4.6 Protecting devs from moving goal posts.

It’s usually the devs who figure out that the goalposts need to move.

11.4.7 Need sprints to have retros

Retros are just moments of introspection. Monks have been dedicating their entire lives to introspection since long before sprints were invented.

11.4.8 Non-technical scrum-master

Usually means PO ends up serving as scrum-master.

11.4.9 Breaking out of stand-up

You can’t tell how much longer a discussion will go on for and it’s hard to decide to exclude people from a discussion. So go in order of urgency, not people.

11.4.10 Delayed response

Average 1.5 sprints before the simplest request is delivered. Turns little hiccups into crises.

11.5 Scrum variations

11.5.1 Everybody is invited to every standup and demo (SAFe)

Minor advantage for huge time limitation.

11.5.2 “Shape Up” process

Doubles down on worst mistakes of scrum:

  • Bigger gong
  • Customer assumed pliable
  • Wild assumptions about nature of project, esp. size of deliverable features
  • Nobody touch the fire extinguisher

Favours flexing scope over schedule or quality, but fails to create the conditions that allow this, namely a prepared range of luxury levels.
